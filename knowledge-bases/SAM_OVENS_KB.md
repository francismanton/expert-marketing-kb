CATEGORY: Strategy / Market Reality
---

CORE CONCEPT:
The online business market has fundamentally shifted. What worked two years ago -- cheap Facebook ads, fly-by-night funnels, email broadcasting -- no longer works. Traffic prices are up, conversion rates are down, email list responsiveness has deteriorated. The market now demands higher quality, real expertise, and genuine problem-solving rather than slick marketing over thin products.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:

- Email list broadcasting is in decline. Responsiveness has deteriorated rapidly. If your entire model depends on blasting an email list, diversify your customer acquisition immediately.
- SEO, organic social media, and blogging have all lost effectiveness as standalone acquisition channels. The bar for content quality and volume has risen beyond what most solo operators can sustain.
- The market is saturated with fly-by-night operators copying each other's funnels and ads. Differentiation now comes from actually being excellent at what you deliver, not from marketing tricks.
- Businesses that survive this shift will be the ones solving real problems with genuine expertise, not those running copied funnels with watered-down products.
- Stop looking for easy, cheap, fast tactics. The market has matured and now rewards depth, commitment, and real skill.

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when a client is struggling with rising ad costs and declining funnel performance, or when someone is trying to enter the market with a copycat funnel and low-quality offer. This is the "wake up call" framework -- the market has changed and your strategy must change with it.

TITLE: Ninja Shit -- Playing To The Biases Of Facebook's Algorithm
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6acYyOoIdwM
CATEGORY: Ads / Facebook Algorithm
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Facebook's algorithm has biases and tendencies that can be exploited if you understand how it thinks. Rather than fighting the algorithm or treating it as a black box, you can structure your campaigns, bidding, creative, and audience targeting to align with what the algorithm wants to reward -- which is engagement, relevance, and user experience. Playing to these biases compounds your ad efficiency over time.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Facebook's algorithm prioritizes ads that generate positive user engagement (reactions, comments, shares, saves). Structure your creative to invite engagement rather than just broadcast a message. Ads that generate conversation get rewarded with lower costs.
- The algorithm has a learning phase bias -- it needs roughly 50 conversion events per ad set per week to optimize effectively. If your ad sets are starved of data (too many ad sets, too little budget, too narrow audiences), the algorithm cannot learn and your costs will be volatile.
- Relevance score (now quality ranking, engagement ranking, conversion ranking) directly impacts your CPM. A high-relevance ad to the right audience can cost 2-5x less per impression than a low-relevance ad. Invest time in audience-creative alignment.
- The algorithm responds to consistency. Erratic budget changes, constant campaign restructuring, and frequent pausing/restarting prevent it from optimizing. Let campaigns run with stable budgets long enough for the algorithm to find its rhythm.
- Use social proof strategically. The algorithm factors in engagement history on your ad. Running ads to warm audiences first to accumulate likes/comments, then expanding to cold audiences with that social proof, can significantly improve cold audience performance.
- Campaign Budget Optimization (CBO) works best when your ad sets are of similar audience size. If you put a 500K audience next to a 50M audience in CBO, the algorithm will dump budget into the larger one regardless of performance. Match audience sizes within CBO campaigns.

KEY QUOTES:
- "You want to play to the biases of the algorithm, not fight against it."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when Facebook ad costs are rising without clear reason, when campaigns are stuck in learning phase, or when a client is constantly restructuring campaigns and seeing volatile results. This is the "work with the machine, not against it" framework.

Ovens provides the conceptual understanding of WHY the algorithm behaves as it does; Haynes provides the specific tactical playbooks for exploiting those behaviors at scale.

TITLE: Horizontal Scaling -- How To Scale Facebook Ads To The Moon
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qQOSOzMmFuA
CATEGORY: Ads / Scaling
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Horizontal scaling is the method for taking Facebook ad spend from hundreds per day to $5K, $10K, or $40K+ per day. Rather than simply increasing budget on existing campaigns (vertical scaling, which triggers the learning phase and destabilizes performance), horizontal scaling involves creating new parallel campaigns, ad sets, audiences, creatives, angles, and geographic targets. Eight specific methods can be deployed in sequence as you push spend higher.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Method 1 -- Duplicate Ad Sets at Max Budget: Take your best-performing ad set, duplicate it multiple times, and set each at a budget level where it performs well. Instead of one ad set at $5,000/day, run five ad sets at $1,000/day each targeting the same audience. This avoids learning phase resets.
- Method 2 -- New Audience Interests: Expand by finding new interest-based audiences to target. Use Audience Insights and lateral thinking to find adjacent interest targets. If "Tony Robbins" works, test "Brendon Burchard," "Dean Graziosi," etc.
- Method 3 -- Lookalike Audiences: Create 1%, 2%, 3%, up to 10% lookalikes from your best custom audiences (purchasers, high-value customers, engaged video viewers). Lookalikes from purchaser lists are typically the highest-performing cold audiences.
- Method 4 -- New Ad Variations: Create new visual variations (different images, video thumbnails, color schemes, formats) of your best-performing ads. Same message, different packaging.
- Method 5 -- New Ad Angles: Go deeper than visual changes -- create entirely new messaging angles, hooks, and value propositions. Different pain points, different aspirations, different proof mechanisms.
- Method 6 -- Target Additional Countries: Once you've proven the offer in your primary market, expand to English-speaking countries (UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand), then consider translated campaigns for other markets.
- Method 7 -- Scale Retargeting: As cold traffic increases, your retargeting pools grow proportionally. Build retargeting campaigns for website visitors, video viewers, engagement audiences, and segment them by recency (1-day, 3-day, 7-day, 14-day, 30-day).
- Method 8 -- Decentralized Architecture: At very high spend levels ($10K+/day), run multiple ad accounts, each with its own campaigns, to spread risk and avoid single points of failure. If one account gets restricted, others keep running.

KEY QUOTES:
- "I'm not just talking about how to scale from a hundred dollars a day to five hundred dollars a day... I'm talking how you can scale to five grand a day, ten grand a day, all the way to forty thousand dollars a day in spend."
- "Everything I've learned spending millions of dollars on ads, making tens of millions of dollars from Facebook ads over seven years... I'm gonna boil it down and crystallize it."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when a client has a proven funnel (positive ROAS at $100-500/day) and wants to scale aggressively. Work through the eight methods in order. Also reference when a client is hitting a scaling ceiling -- they're usually stuck because they're trying to vertically scale (just increase budget) rather than horizontally scale (create new parallel pathways for spend).

CROSS-REFERENCE (Jeremy Haynes): Haynes' Hydra strategy is essentially a branded version of Methods 1-3 here. His emphasis on decentralized architecture and multiple payment methods aligns directly with Method 8. Ovens provides the systematic framework; Haynes adds specific naming conventions and tactical nuances like credit line management (Chase/Citi backup for when Amex hits limits).

## SECTION 3: SYSTEMS THINKING

TITLE: The Flywheel Secret Behind Amazon, Google, Uber
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u6dXN58rB2k
CATEGORY: Systems / Business Models
---

CORE CONCEPT:
There are two types of business growth: linear and exponential. Linear businesses grow by doing more of the same (double input, double output). Exponential businesses have a flywheel -- a self-reinforcing cycle where each new customer or unit of activity makes the entire system better. Amazon, Google, Uber, Facebook, and Airbnb all have flywheels. Most service businesses (done-for-you, one-on-one consulting) have ANTI-flywheels where each new customer degrades the experience.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- The flywheel test: For every one new customer added, does the experience get better or worse? If better, you have a flywheel. If worse, you have an anti-flywheel. Most service businesses fail this test.
- Amazon's flywheel: More sellers = wider selection = better customer experience = more traffic = more sellers. Plus: scale lowers costs = lower prices = better experience = more traffic. Every element reinforces every other element.
- Done-for-you and one-on-one consulting models have an inherent anti-flywheel. More clients = less time per client = worse results = fewer referrals/new clients. Adding more humans to deliver the work does NOT solve this -- it makes it less efficient.
- To build a flywheel in a training/consulting business: (a) Use a Facebook group or community where more members means faster answers and better peer support. (b) Build a content archive -- the longer you operate, the deeper your content library, the better the experience for new members. (c) Build search functionality with an algorithm that improves with usage.
- Stop forcing students/clients into carbon-copy niches. When you tell everyone to "do Facebook ads for dentists," you create an anti-flywheel -- market saturation degrades results for everyone. Instead, let each person combine your program (X) with their unique background (Y) to create unique X+Y combinations that cannot be replicated.
- The DNA metaphor: Bad programs produce X+X clones (everyone identical). Good programs produce X+Y hybrids where Y is each person's unique DNA. This creates diversity, which creates a flywheel because no two members compete directly.

KEY QUOTES:
- "For every one customer the experience must get better -- that's what makes [a flywheel] happen."
- "If you have a machine where with every new customer the experience gets worse, the worst thing you can do is try and get more customers."
- "Anyone who claims that they know the top niches in the world... doesn't understand how life and the world works."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when designing or evaluating a business model. Ask the flywheel question: does each new customer make the system better? If the answer is no, restructuring the delivery model is more important than getting more leads. Also reference when a client is running a group program and forcing everyone into identical niches -- they're building an anti-flywheel.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Jeremy Haynes): Haynes' emphasis on multiple funnel types running simultaneously is a form of horizontal diversification that partially addresses the anti-flywheel problem. His show rate optimization and setter/closer model are attempts to maintain service quality at scale -- directly relevant to Ovens' anti-flywheel warning about one-on-one models degrading with volume.

TITLE: Systems Thinking -- How Billionaires Think
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rcxiCDNHJpM
CATEGORY: Systems / Mental Models
---

CORE CONCEPT:
The core difference between high performers and everyone else is that high performers are systems thinkers. They see everything as interconnected systems (inputs, processes, outputs, feedback loops, environment), while most people are component thinkers who see isolated parts without understanding how those parts affect the whole. This single shift in thinking -- from component to system -- transforms decision-making in business and life.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Every system has five elements: INPUTS (what goes in), PROCESSES (what happens to inputs), OUTPUTS (what comes out), FEEDBACK (how outputs affect future inputs), and ENVIRONMENT (external forces that influence the system). Use this framework to analyze anything.
- Component thinking trap: Optimizing a landing page conversion rate from 15% to 40% can HURT overall business performance. Higher opt-in rates often mean lower-quality leads, which means lower sales conversion, lower customer quality, and lower lifetime value. A systems thinker measures the WHOLE funnel (cost per acquisition, ROAS), not individual components.
- Customer support is not a cost center -- it's a sales tool. Bad support kills repeat purchases and referrals. Ovens tracks customer happiness score (targeting 87%+, aiming to beat Google's 90%) and response time (currently ~2.5 hours, goal is 6 minutes). Invest in support quality because it feeds back into revenue.
- Your personal system: If you want high-quality output (good work, focus, creativity), your inputs must be high-quality (sleep, nutrition, good information via books). Garbage in, garbage out. Twenty beers as input = zero productive output + days of recovery.
- Feedback loops drive exponential behavior. The biggest influence on a stock price is the stock price itself (going up attracts more buyers, which pushes it up further). In your business, good customer results = more referrals = more customers = more good results. This is why customer outcomes matter so much -- they create the feedback loop that drives growth.
- Emergence vs. silos: Don't force everyone into identical paths. Set simple rules and let complex, valuable behavior emerge. Example: Stop telling students which niche to pick. Let them choose based on their unique interests. This produces niches like "porn addiction consulting" and "irritable bowel syndrome consulting" -- uniquely profitable because no one else is competing there.
- The trades vs. lawyers paradox: When everyone was told to become lawyers/accountants and avoid trades, the supply of trades dried up and the supply of lawyers flooded. Now plumbers and electricians in New Zealand earn more than lawyers. Systems thinkers understand that pushing everyone into the same path destroys the value of that path.

KEY QUOTES:
- "Most successful people... view everything as a system... not linear cause and effect."
- "The person who looks at their landing page and thinks that by improving their landing page conversion rate they're improving their whole business -- not true. I've seen the opposite is true a lot of the time."
- "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it'll dictate your life and you will call it fate."
- "If I put $1 in a machine and $3 comes out, why would I limit my putting of dollars into the machine?"

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this constantly. This is Sam Ovens' foundational mental model. Use it when: diagnosing why a funnel isn't working (measure the whole system, not one component), when a client is optimizing the wrong metric, when someone thinks customer support doesn't affect revenue, when personal productivity is low (check inputs -- sleep, diet, information consumption), or when designing any business process.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Jeremy Haynes): Haynes' "million-dollar math" framework (work backwards from $1M to required customer count, calls, show rate, ad spend) is a practical application of systems thinking. His emphasis on measuring 30-day cash collected rather than projected revenue is a systems-level metric, not a component-level one.

TITLE: A Recipe For Domination -- Long Term Thinking, Moats
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PdTBLPL2yos
CATEGORY: Strategy / Competitive Advantage
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Most entrepreneurs ask "what is something cheap, easy, and fast that gives us an advantage?" Billionaires ask the opposite: "what is something expensive, time-consuming, and excruciatingly hard that gives us an advantage?" Easy advantages are quickly copied and neutralized. Hard advantages -- moats -- are what create lasting dominance. There are four moat categories: excellence at your craft, systems, brand, team, and proprietary technology.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Moat Category 1 -- Excellence: Be the best at what you do. Thousands of hours of mastery and practice create an advantage that cannot be replicated quickly. When competitors try to match your skill level and realize how hard it is, they quit. The harder the path you've walked, the more it protects you.
- Moat Category 2 -- Systems: Master one channel (e.g., Facebook ads) and one funnel, and refine them obsessively for years. Ovens spent 4+ years and thousands of hours on a single funnel. Most people won't put 5 years into one thing -- that discipline IS the advantage.
- Moat Category 3 -- Brand: Build a brand that compounds over time. Ovens bought consulting.com (expensive domain) as a long-term brand investment. In the short term it cost money and gave no advantage. Long-term, the SEO, recognition, and trust will be nearly impossible to replicate.
- Moat Category 4 -- Team: Hire in-house rather than outsource. If your ads person is a contractor who works for everyone, your ads will be as good as everyone else's. An in-house person trained to be exceptional gives you an advantage no competitor can hire away. Contractors and licensed software make your business fully replicable overnight.
- Moat Category 5 -- Proprietary Technology: Build your own tools when off-the-shelf solutions aren't good enough. Ovens built custom multi-channel attribution (cost: $1M+, time: 18 months) because nothing on the market could accurately attribute sales across YouTube, Facebook, Instagram, etc. This gives better data, better decisions, and an edge no competitor can buy.
- Amazon's moat explained: Not the website or SEO -- it's the fulfillment network (factories, ships, airlines, fleets). Cost: hundreds of billions. Time: 25 years. Employees: 600,000+. No startup can replicate this. They also went 25 years without profit, reinvesting everything while competitors needed profits to fund their lifestyles.
- Asymmetric warfare: Don't play by the same rules as your competitors. Amazon didn't try to build more physical stores than Walmart -- Bezos read "Made in America" by Sam Walton and built an ONLINE Walmart. Different rules, different game, competitive advantage.
- The lifestyle trap: Competitors who need profits to fund Lamborghini payments and Instagram photos are vulnerable. If you reinvest profits while they extract them, you will eventually crush them and absorb their market share.

KEY QUOTES:
- "People like Jeff Bezos... say what is something we can do that is expensive, takes forever, and is excruciatingly hard that gives us an advantage."
- "The harder it is, the more grueling it is, and the more gnarly the problem is that you battle through and conquer -- the cool thing is that you know that everybody else has to go through that whole fire to get to where you are."
- "Most people are only willing to put a day or a week of work into something. Sometimes I'll put 12 hours a day of work for four years straight into one thing."
- "What's popular is often wrong, and what hasn't been done is often the things that make greatness."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when a client is looking for quick fixes, copying competitors' funnels, or using all-contractor teams and off-the-shelf tools. This is the "think bigger and harder" framework. Also reference when a client is worried about competitors -- help them identify what they can do that's so hard/expensive/time-consuming that competitors won't bother.

TITLE: Stop Confusing Yourself -- Define The Problem And Solve It
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgetBrY7lhE
CATEGORY: Strategy / Problem Solving / Mindset
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Most entrepreneurs are confused, anxious, and unproductive because they haven't defined what they want. Without a clear objective, you cannot choose the right action, and without the right action you get nowhere. Einstein said if he had one hour to solve a problem, he'd spend 55 minutes defining it and 5 minutes solving it. Most problem-solving is problem-defining -- once a problem is properly defined, it's nearly solved.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Step 1: Define what you want. If you're confused about what to do, it's because you haven't defined what you're trying to achieve. Take alone time -- away from social media, phone, and other people -- and write down your objective clearly.
- Step 2: Identify what's stopping you. Once the objective is clear, ask "what's stopping me from achieving this?" Often the answer is "nothing -- I actually know what to do" and the clarity alone resolves the paralysis.
- Step 3: Find someone who has achieved this objective (or gotten close) and study what means they used. That means becomes your action plan.
- The confusion test on Q&A calls: Confused people tell stories and don't ask questions, or they say "so what do you think?" Clear people ask specific questions. If you can't articulate your question, you haven't defined your problem.
- Business at first principles: Pick a niche based on passion/curiosity (not logic). Research the people in that niche. Understand their problems. Find a common, widespread problem. Figure out how to solve it. Offer the solution for money. That's it.
- The real obstacle is usually not tactical: Ovens found that students who didn't succeed weren't failing because the course needed more tactics. They failed because their lives were too chaotic -- listening to 50 people, subscribed to 19 email lists, two pages into 19 books, in 6 courses, running 4 businesses. The solution was to help them clean up their lives first.
- Focus is the inverse of distraction: Instead of trying harder to focus (which requires unsustainable willpower), eliminate distractions. Delete apps, unsubscribe from email lists, put phone on airplane mode. Ovens runs his entire business from one screen with 4-5 apps on his phone.
- The fame trap: People confuse being famous on social media with being successful. These are different skills. Someone with a fancy car on Instagram isn't necessarily good at what they do -- they're good at being famous. Don't model your business after their social media strategy.

KEY QUOTES:
- "If I had one hour to solve a problem, I'd spend 55 minutes defining it and five minutes solving it." (Einstein, cited by Ovens)
- "A very clear sign that somebody is confused is when they don't ask a question and they tell a story."
- "Instead of getting people to try and focus, I just got them to get rid of distraction."
- "Smart phones, social media, and alcohol -- probably the biggest distractions and most toxic things for you if you're trying to succeed."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when a client is scattered, doing everything but achieving nothing, or can't articulate what they're trying to accomplish. Start every engagement by defining the objective. Also reference when someone is drowning in tactics and courses but not executing -- the problem is probably not a knowledge gap but a clarity/distraction problem.

TITLE: Tools To Plan For The New Year
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l6lV30ds3XI
CATEGORY: Planning / Systems / Execution
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Strategic planning is a four-tool system: (1) Vision, Mission, and Principles define your 30-year direction, (2) a War Map Calendar breaks that vision into yearly, monthly, and daily actions, (3) a RICE Project Scoring tool prioritizes which projects to tackle in what order, and (4) a Health Tracking Sheet optimizes your personal inputs (sleep, diet, exercise, focus) to maximize output toward your vision. All four tools interconnect -- everything derives from the Vision/Mission/Principles.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Tool 1 -- Vision, Mission, Principles: Start by defining the problem you see in the world, then your vision (what the world looks like 20-30 years out because of your work), then your mission (how you plan to get there), then your principles (fundamental beliefs that govern all decisions). Frame it on one page and put it on your desk.
- Why small goals fail: Setting goals like "get a Ferrari" or "$100K/year" works in the short term but provides no lasting motivation, can't attract talented team members, and leads to plateauing. Set your sights on something bigger than yourself.
- Company examples: Apple -- "Make tools for the mind that advance humankind." Microsoft -- "A computer on every desk and in every home." Amazon, Google, Tesla, SpaceX all started from a clearly defined problem and an ambitious vision.
- Tool 2 -- War Map Calendar: Plot 5-10 annual goals derived from your vision. Map when each project will be executed across the year. Plan monthly milestones 30 days out, daily actions only 7-14 days out, and 30-minute increments the night before each day. At 9:30 PM each night, plan exactly what tomorrow looks like in 30-minute blocks so you wake up needing zero thinking -- only action.
- Tool 3 -- RICE Scoring: Score each potential project on Reach (how many customers impacted), Impact (how much it moves the needle toward your mission), Confidence (how sure you are it'll work), and Effort (person-months required). The algorithm produces a priority score. Higher score = do it first.
- Tool 4 -- Health Tracking Sheet: Track daily: sleep times, sleep quality (using Oura Ring -- $299, recommended), alertness, mood, soreness, meditation (20 min/day recommended), diet quality (1-3 scale), supplements, workout, work start/end times, output quality, focus score, whether you planned tomorrow today, and whether you read.
- The planning hierarchy: 30-year vision > annual goals > quarterly milestones > monthly goals > weekly goals > daily objectives > 30-minute action blocks. Each level derives from the one above it. Nothing happens that isn't connected back to the vision.
- Consulting.com's principles as example: Students obsessed, laser focused, high standards ("great just isn't good enough"), long-term thinking, lean and frugal, scientific method, go all in, full transparency, question everything, invent.

KEY QUOTES:
- "Small gets small. Big gets big. Whatever you set the intent on, it basically becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy."
- "I don't believe in the law of attraction the way people think... I believe in the law of action."
- "Luck is the residue of good design."
- "If not now, when? And if not you, who?"

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this at the start of any new year, quarter, or major planning cycle. Use it when a client lacks direction, is working hard but on the wrong things, or has a team that's pulling in different directions. The Vision/Mission/Principles framework is the starting point for all organizational alignment. The RICE scoring tool is the antidote to "I have too many ideas and don't know which to prioritize."

Where Ovens provides the strategic planning architecture, Haynes provides the specific marketing calendar to fill it with.

TITLE: Sacrifice, Yield, And Asymmetric Returns
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O5qolFKg64s
CATEGORY: Mindset / Productivity / Sacrifice
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Every human has the same two resources: time and energy. The distribution of these resources is roughly equal across all people, yet the distribution of results is wildly skewed. The difference is where people choose to allocate time and energy. Building anything great requires sacrifice -- systematically identifying and eliminating activities that don't move you toward your goal, including many activities that feel productive but aren't.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- The constraint equation: You are the true constraint. You can't perform concurrent operations or be replicated. Life = energy over time. Where you put it determines what you get.
- The sacrifice evaluation framework: For every activity, ask three questions: (1) Does it make progress toward my goal? (2) Does it make no difference? (3) Does it cause regression? If it's not #1, cut it.
- The 100 Units of Time exercise: Set an alarm every 30-60 minutes for 7 days. Write down exactly what you were doing each time it buzzes. Be brutally honest. After 7 days, categorize all activities and calculate the percentage of time spent on each category. This baseline audit reveals where your time actually goes vs. where you think it goes.
- Phone usage as the lowest-hanging sacrifice: Ovens made his mastermind members check their screen time -- some were at 5 hours/day. His own is ~21 minutes (20 of which is meditation). Getting an Android (worse UI than iPhone) was an intentional friction strategy to reduce phone use.
- Three biggest time/energy destroyers: smartphones, social media, and alcohol. Alcohol has a "long half-life" -- one night out costs a week of reduced performance through hangover, depression, guilt, and sickness (second, third, fourth-order consequences).
- Decisions have cascading consequences: Getting drunk isn't just the hours lost that night. It's the hangover, the depression about what you did, the sickness, the missed work days. Learn to see second, third, fourth-order consequences of every choice.
- Sacrifice is not a one-time event: Repeat the 100 Units exercise every 1-2 months because your responsibilities and activities change. Even when you're already efficient, the next level comes from "trading winners for better winners" -- removing less constructive activities for more constructive ones.
- Real-world examples of focus: Leonardo DiCaprio doesn't use social media or do interviews -- he just makes exceptional movies. The best basketball players aren't the ones with the most Instagram followers. "Winning speaks louder than photos and likes."
- You must define the goal before you can optimize: You can't evaluate which activities to sacrifice until you know what you're optimizing for. This is exactly like a Facebook conversion pixel -- you need to define the conversion event before the algorithm can optimize.
