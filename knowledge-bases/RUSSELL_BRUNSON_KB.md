TITLE: What Is A Sales Funnel And How To Create One
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CM-RAMN_mgY
CATEGORY: Funnels
---

CORE CONCEPT:
A sales funnel is the online evolution of a website — a structured sequence of pages designed to increase the average revenue per visitor through upsells and downsells. The governing principle (borrowed from Dan Kennedy) is "whoever can spend the most money to acquire a customer wins," and funnels are the mechanism that makes higher acquisition spend profitable.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- A basic website with a single product is fragile to traffic cost increases. Brunson's potato gun DVD example: $10/day ads producing $37/sale was profitable, but when ad costs rose to $50/day he went broke — the product couldn't cover the traffic.
- Adding a single upsell (potato gun kit at $197, converting 1 in 3 buyers) changed the math: $50 spend now produced $102/day ($37 DVD + $65 average upsell revenue). That margin difference is the entire case for funnels over websites.
- Funnels are not mysterious or new — every offline business already has one (walk in door, greeted by receptionist, scheduled for appointment, upsold services). Online funnels replicate this same sequential process digitally.
- There are 22+ funnel types in the ClickFunnels "cookbook" — some for selling products, some for generating leads, some for booking calls, some for driving in-store visits. Match the funnel type to the business objective.
- The exercise: take your existing website/product lineup and restructure it into a funnel sequence. Ask "what's the next logical thing someone needs after buying the first thing?"

KEY QUOTES:
- "Whoever can spend the most money to acquire a customer wins."
- "Websites made me broke but funnels actually made me money."

APPLY WHEN:
Use this framework when a client has a single-product website and is struggling with traffic costs, or when explaining to a non-technical business owner why they need a funnel instead of (or layered on top of) a traditional website. This is foundational — reference it before diving into specific funnel types.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Haynes): Haynes' "million-dollar math" framework is the quantitative version of Brunson's "spend the most to acquire" principle. Both arrive at the same conclusion — you need to engineer unit economics that let you outspend competitors — but Haynes operationalizes it with specific cost-per-call and close-rate calculations.

TITLE: Sales Funnel Strategy: 7 Simple Hacks
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ip59Qbv6J_w
CATEGORY: Funnels
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Seven specific, high-leverage tweaks that can be applied to any existing funnel to increase revenue — most are small changes (headline rewrites, page structure, post-purchase behavior) rather than full rebuilds. The throughline is that funnel optimization is usually about fixing one or two small things, not starting over.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Hack 1 — Add curiosity to headlines: A webinar registration page changed from explaining exactly what the webinar teaches to a curiosity-driven "what it's NOT" format. Result: ad cost dropped from $24.85 to $5.84 per registrant, and show-up rate jumped from 22% to over 30%. The principle: if people know what you're going to teach, they won't show up because they think they already know it.
- Hack 2 — Use a lead magnet to grow your list: Even a 2-paragraph "Kiss Test" freebie built a $20M/year business (Double Your Dating). The lead magnet doesn't need to be a 55-minute video or 39-page ebook — it needs to deliver one compelling insight, then push to the next step in the value ladder.
- Hack 3 — Structure sales pages as Emotion > Logic > Fear: Top third of the page is emotional (story, hook, video). Middle section is logical (bullet points, what you'll learn). Bottom section is urgency/scarcity. ~90% of buyers convert in the emotional section. Scrollers are logical buyers. Bottom-of-page buyers need a fear-based push. Apply this same sequence to email sequences.
- Hack 4 — Add an order form bump: After credit card entry but before the submit button, offer an impulse buy ($17-$37) — audiobook version, templates, etc. Must be instantly understood (not a multi-paragraph explanation). In most funnels, the order form bump alone covers ad spend, making everything else pure profit.
- Hack 5 — Keep the buying loop open on upsell pages: Never say "Thank you for your order" on an upsell page — it closes the buying loop and conversions drop dramatically. Instead: "Wait, your order's not yet complete!" or "Don't shut this page down!" One consulting client changed only this language and saw dramatic conversion increases.
- Hack 6 — Add an offer wall to the Thank You page: After purchase completion, show links to other products/value ladders. Hyperactive buyers will look for more things to buy — if you don't offer them, they'll buy from competitors.
- Hack 7 — Put a webinar on the Thank You page: Start training immediately while the product ships. A percentage of $7 book buyers convert to $2,000 products sold via the Thank You page webinar.

KEY QUOTES:
- "Don't tell me what it is, tell me what it's not."
- "After they put in their credit card, but before they click the submit button — that's where the magic happens."
- "Your order's not yet complete!"

APPLY WHEN:
Use this as a diagnostic checklist when a client's funnel exists but underperforms. Run through all seven hacks and identify which ones are missing. Particularly valuable for clients who want to "fix" their funnel but don't know where to start — these are the highest-ROI micro-optimizations.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Haynes): Haynes emphasizes show rates as a critical lever (his dedicated show-rate content). Brunson's Hack 1 (curiosity headlines) directly addresses show rates from the registration side. Haynes' approach focuses more on post-registration nurture sequences; combined, they cover the full show-rate optimization stack.

TITLE: Fastest Way To Make Money With ClickBank
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kRR3zdzeG9k
CATEGORY: Traffic / Affiliate Strategy
---

CORE CONCEPT:
For people without their own product, affiliate marketing through ClickBank using a bridge page funnel is the fastest path to revenue. The bridge page acts as your personal endorsement layer between the traffic source and the affiliate product, and the real business value comes from owning the email list — not the initial affiliate commission.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Build an opt-in page with a curiosity-based headline (CBH) first, then a bridge page, then push to the affiliate product. The opt-in captures their email address before they ever see the affiliate offer — this is non-negotiable because the list is the asset.
- The bridge page should be a personal testimonial or case study video. Structure: headline hook + video telling your story with the product + Brunson Box (bullet-point teaser of what's in the video for non-watchers) + call to action.
- Differentiate from the 500 other affiliates promoting the same product by adding bonuses. "If you buy through my link, I'll also send you [bonus 1], [bonus 2], [bonus 3]." Fulfillment: have buyers send you their receipt, then you deliver the bonuses. This is what separates "a little money" from "a lot."
- The break-even point is the key metric. You might lose money on day 1 (spend $1, make $0.75 in affiliate commissions). But by day 5 of your email sequence — promoting related affiliate products (Pruvit, Organifi, etc.) — you break even. Everything after that is pure profit.
- Take buyers on a customer journey: diet product buyer gets offered keto drinks, then green supplements, then fitness programs. Each email promotes a complementary affiliate offer. The lifetime value of the list far exceeds the initial commission.

KEY QUOTES:
- "The value in any business is the customer."
- "Somewhere between here and the end of your customer journey, which if you treat people right will be forever, there's this magic spot called the break-even point."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when working with someone who has no product yet but wants to start generating revenue online, or when building an affiliate funnel strategy. Also useful as a mental model for any business: the bridge page concept applies universally — you're always the bridge between a prospect's current state and the solution.

NOTE: This video is a live build session with Jim Edwards and Julie — roughly half is demo/screen-sharing of building pages in ClickFunnels. The strategic content is front-loaded in the whiteboard segment.

TITLE: How To Sell Anything With An Irresistible Offer
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AbDMNSfOY6c
CATEGORY: Offers
---

CORE CONCEPT:
An offer is not a product — it's a bundle of value that makes price comparison impossible. Every step of a funnel (the ad, the landing page, the upsell) requires its own offer. If your funnel isn't converting, the most likely problem is that your offer isn't compelling enough, and you may need to test 4-6 different offers before finding the one that resonates.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- The supplement example: same supplement sold on Amazon for $19.95/bottle vs. Brunson selling it for $67/bottle bundled with a weight loss guide and calorie tracker. The bundle makes it a different category — no longer a commodity competing on price.
- Every step in the funnel has its own offer. The ad's offer is curiosity/pain relief ("find out how to get rid of your pain in 3 weeks"). The landing page offer is information for email address ("give me your email, I'll send you this 6-page report"). The sales page offer is the product bundle. Each step trades value for something (attention, email, money).
- "Funnel hacking" offers: study what competitors and adjacent businesses offer at each step. Look at their landing page offer, upsell offer, etc. Not to copy — but to understand what structures and bundles are working in the market.
- If your funnel isn't working and traffic is good, the offer is the first thing to examine. "If your offer sucks, you need to crank it up, make it more sexy." Expect to iterate 4-6 times before finding the winning offer.
- The homework: take every page in your funnel and define the specific offer at each step. What value are you providing? What are you asking for in return?

KEY QUOTES:
- "An offer is not a product."
- "If your offer sucks, you need to crank it up, make it more sexy."

APPLY WHEN:
Use this when a client's funnel has decent traffic but poor conversion at any step — opt-in rate, sales page, or upsell. The first diagnostic question should be "what's your offer at this step?" Also use when a client is selling a commodity product and competing on price — they need to bundle into an offer.

TITLE: How To Create A Mastermind Group
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SnU5IUIo7yU
CATEGORY: Strategy / Community
---

CORE CONCEPT:
A mastermind group is both a high-ticket revenue model and an accelerator for all members' businesses. The key operational decisions — topic niche, member selection, capping membership, and high-energy facilitation format — determine whether it becomes a transformative community or a boring seminar that people leave.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Define your niche cross-section: Brunson's Inner Circle = funnels. Stu's = membership sites. Rhonda Swan's = branding + travel (Bali location). The intersection of two interests creates uniqueness that attracts the right people.
- Gate with an application process. Kick out takers who don't contribute. Cap membership (Brunson capped at 100) — this creates scarcity ("Inner Circle for life — if you leave, you can never come back") and prevents churn. The cap also builds family-like bonds as the same people re-up year after year.
- Meet in person twice per year. Randomize seating every session — don't let people sit with friends. Discomfort creates vulnerability and new connections.
- Format: Each member gets 30 minutes on stage. First 10-15 minutes: share what's working (provide value first). Last 15 minutes: ask the group for help. Then facilitator calls "walk and talk" — everyone stands, grabs a partner, shares their biggest takeaway from that presentation. This keeps energy high and cements learning.
- Energy management is critical: DJ between sessions, throwable microphones (no mic runners), paleo food (no sugar crashes), energy drinks, energy gum from the military, standing breaks between every session. "If you run a mastermind like school, everybody falls asleep."
- End with a round-robin: everyone shares their single biggest takeaway. Different people extract different insights from the same presentations — hearing others' takeaways often reshuffles your own priorities.
- The "aftermind": post-event networking continues into the evening and weekend, building deeper relationships.

KEY QUOTES:
- "You are the average of your five closest friends."
- "Every one of these notebooks has made me at least an extra million dollars." (Member Jamie Amus holding up 4 notebooks from 4 meetings)

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when a client is considering launching a high-ticket community/mastermind as the top of their value ladder, or when they need operational details on how to run one. Also useful as a model for any recurring high-ticket offer — the principles of capping, application gating, and energy management apply to any premium group program.

NOTE: This is a produced mini-documentary style video, heavier on storytelling than tactical frameworks. The operational details above are embedded throughout the narrative.

TITLE: How To 5X Your Business With Dan Kennedy
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khw9FlZY5Bg
CATEGORY: Strategy / Direct Response
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Dan Kennedy's core direct response principles — message-to-market match, the economics of customer acquisition, personality-driven marketing, and plugging internal revenue leaks — are the foundation Brunson built ClickFunnels on. This interview surfaces Kennedy's frameworks in his own words, with Brunson providing the modern digital context.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Message-to-market match is the #1 determinant of marketing success. Kennedy's example: an orthodontist running Invisalign ads that drive to a generic orthodontist website instantly disappoints the prospect. Every offer needs its own complete marketing system — "never the twain shall meet." The tighter the match between who you're targeting and what they see, the stronger the magnetism.
- The "welcome guest vs. annoying pest" framework: the only difference between the two is whether your message matches what someone needs right now. Kennedy's literal story — someone banging on his door was an annoying pest until they said "your shrubbery is on fire," at which point they became a most welcome guest. Right message + right time = magnetic marketing.
- "Whoever can spend the most to acquire a customer wins" (Kennedy's original quote that Brunson adopted): In competitive environments, the business that figures out how to spend the most per lead and per sale buys speed, scale, discouragement of competition, and sustainability. When Kennedy started in infomercials, airtime was $200/half-hour. Five years later it was $20,000. The businesses that survived were the ones that engineered their economics to afford it.
- "Nothing overcomes bad economics": No amount of great copy, amazing product, or brilliant funnel design compensates for math that doesn't work. Marketing is behavioral psychology AND math — most people love the psychology and ignore the math.
- Personality-driven marketing: Kennedy's three models are Walt Disney, Stan Lee (Marvel), and Hugh Hefner (Playboy). All three created a world for their customers — media they owned and controlled, a community identity, and a personality at the center. Stan Lee broke the comic book rule of never acknowledging fiction, let fans in on the creative process, and built a tribe. Key insight: "You don't get free, rich, or secure by what you do — you get it by what you own."
- Finding internal revenue leaks: A chiropractic office that lets all front desk staff go to lunch simultaneously (phones go to voicemail) loses 20% of gross revenue from that one decision. Find 5 internal and 5 external leaks like this and you can "double double" the business. After acquiring Kennedy's company, Brunson 5x'd newsletter subscribers "in a matter of weeks" by fixing the funnel structure.

KEY QUOTES:
- "More than half has to do with the audience that you've selected, and if you've selected really well, mediocre marketing will often get extraordinary results."
- "Nothing overcomes bad economics."
- "You don't get free, rich, or secure by what you do — you get it by what you own."
- "The only thing that changed was right message, right time."

APPLY WHEN:
Reference this when a client has good marketing execution but poor targeting (message-to-market mismatch), when unit economics don't work and they're trying to "creative" their way out of bad math, or when building a personality-driven brand. Also relevant when doing a business audit for revenue leaks — look for the "phones on voicemail at lunch" equivalent in every business.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Haynes): Kennedy's "nothing overcomes bad economics" aligns directly with Haynes' insistence on doing the "million-dollar math" before building anything. Both reject the idea that great creative or funnel design can save fundamentally broken unit economics. Haynes' content-first ad strategies (Venus Fly Trap, Harvester) are modern implementations of Kennedy's message-to-market match principle.

TITLE: Number 1 Way To Make More Money: Value Ladder
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZRGczmUFH2Y
CATEGORY: Strategy / Offers
---

CORE CONCEPT:
The Value Ladder is Brunson's core business architecture framework: customers enter at a low-cost/free level, receive value, and naturally ascend to higher-priced offers as trust builds. Every funnel is a micro value ladder (each page is a step), and the overall business is a macro value ladder (each funnel feeds into the next). You cannot sell a $1M offer to someone who has never received value from you.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- The Value Ladder has two axes: value (vertical) and price (horizontal). As you provide more value, you charge more. The progression: free content > free + shipping book ($7.95) > home study course > live event > coaching > done-for-you service ($1M in Brunson's case).
- You can't start at the top. Brunson's example: "If I walked up to you on the street and said 'give me a million dollars and I'll build you a funnel,' you'd think I'm insane." Trust and demonstrated value must precede high-ticket asks. This is the dating analogy — you ask for a first date, not a marriage proposal.
- Each page in a funnel IS the value ladder in miniature. Page 1: "Give me your email, I'll give you this report" (free value for contact info). Page 2: "Your report is coming — while you're here, here's a special offer on my book" ($7.95). Page 3: "You bought the book — want the home study course?" And so on.
- The Value Ladder works identically in e-commerce. Brunson's example: a tactical flashlight funnel. Buy flashlight > "Want a second one at 50% off?" > "Want a carrying case?" > "Want rush shipping?" Each step provides more value and extracts more revenue.
- People ascend the ladder until you stop offering them something or until you offend them. If you don't have a next step, you're leaving money on the table.
- The reason Brunson can charge $1M for done-for-you funnels: prospects have already received value through books, software, training, and events. Trust, rapport, and demonstrated competence are established across multiple value ladder levels before the high-ticket conversation ever happens.

KEY QUOTES:
- "If I walked up and saw you on the street for the first time and said 'give me a million dollars, I will build a funnel for you' — you're going to think I'm insane."
- "People will ascend up and they'll want more and I can charge them more... they keep moving through this ascension until you stop selling them something or till you offend them."

APPLY WHEN:
Use this as the foundational business architecture exercise for any client. Before building any funnel, map out the full value ladder: what's the free offer? The low-ticket? The mid-ticket? The high-ticket? The ultra-premium? If any level is missing, the business is either leaving money on the table (no high-ticket) or struggling to acquire customers (no low-ticket entry point). This is also the diagnostic for "I can't sell my $10K offer" — check whether the lower rungs of the ladder exist.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Haynes): Haynes' "low-ticket-to-high-ticket" funnel type is a direct implementation of Brunson's value ladder. Haynes operationalizes the ascension with setters who activate low-ticket buyers and closers who upsell them — the tactical sales infrastructure that makes the value ladder work at scale.

TITLE: Your Funnel SUCKS — Here's How To Fix It
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pkfCATQlbrQ
CATEGORY: Funnels / Diagnostics
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Most funnels don't crush it on day one — that's normal, not a failure. The key skill is calling "funnel audibles" — diagnosing what's broken (hook, story, or offer) and making targeted adjustments using data rather than scrapping the entire funnel. Brunson also introduces Geru (funnel simulation software) as a pre-launch diagnostic tool to stress-test economics before spending real money.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- Expect your funnel to underperform at launch. Brunson explicitly tells Inner Circle members before webinar launches: "It's probably going to bomb." Going in with this expectation prevents emotional devastation and enables rational iteration. The first launch generates DATA, not necessarily revenue.
- The diagnostic framework is Hook > Story > Offer. When a funnel underperforms, one (or more) of these three elements is wrong. Diagnose by looking at where people drop off: low click-through on ads = bad hook. People watch but don't buy = bad story or bad offer. People add to cart but don't complete = bad offer or missing urgency.
- Funnel simulation (Geru software): Before building, map out every page and assign estimated conversion rates (landing page at 30%, sales page at 5%, upsell at X%). Input traffic costs per platform (Facebook at $3/click, YouTube at $X). Run the simulation to see if the math works BEFORE spending real money. After launch, plug in actual numbers and re-simulate to identify which page is underperforming vs. projection.
- The "break-even company" concept: Brunson structured Geru as a front-end that runs at break-even, with the goal of feeding customers into ClickFunnels as the backend profit center. This is the same model as his free + shipping books — the front-end exists to acquire customers profitably (or at break-even), not to generate direct profit.
- Build non-personality-dependent front ends. Brunson's strategic concern: "If I get hit by a bus, ClickFunnels struggles because I am the face." Solution: acquire brands (Dan Kennedy, Geru/Doodle/Boomle) that function as independent front ends feeding into the ClickFunnels ecosystem. Each acquisition = a new funnel bringing in customers who don't need to know who Russell Brunson is.
- Agency application: Use funnel simulation to set client expectations before building. Show them: "If this page converts at 1%, here's what happens. If we get it to 3%, here's the profitable scenario." Clients who understand the math before launch don't panic when day-one results require optimization.

KEY QUOTES:
- "Most of the funnels we launch don't just crush it. What normally happens — you build a funnel, you launch it, and it doesn't do that well."
- "It's either the hook, the story, or the offer. One of these three things is wrong."
- "Before you open up ClickFunnels, you gotta do this first — this will change the way you build funnels forever." (On running simulations before building)

APPLY WHEN:
Use this when a client has launched a funnel that isn't working and wants to scrap it. Walk them through Hook > Story > Offer diagnostics instead. Also use the simulation concept during the planning phase — model the economics before investing in build-out. The "break-even company" concept is relevant for any business building front-end acquisition funnels.

CROSS-REFERENCE (Haynes): Haynes' "funnel audibles" presentations align directly — both Brunson and Haynes teach that launch is the beginning of optimization, not the end. Haynes' framework is more metrics-heavy (cost per call, show rate, close rate), while Brunson's Hook > Story > Offer is more creative/messaging-focused. Used together, they cover both the quantitative and qualitative diagnostic spectrum.

TITLE: How I Make An Irresistible Offer
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gPoGjFkwZC4
CATEGORY: Offers
---

CORE CONCEPT:
An irresistible offer is created by stacking value until the price feels absurdly low relative to what's included — Brunson's benchmark is 10x the value of the price. The key mindset shift is moving from selling a commodity (thing everyone else also sells) to selling a unique bundle that can't be price-compared.

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- The 10x value rule: if you're charging $100, the offer should be demonstrably worth $1,000. If it's not, keep stacking until it is. This is the lens for every offer at every level of the value ladder.
- The babysitter example (from Brunson's family event): Every babysitter in town charges $5/hour for the same commodity service. One girl reframed her offer: "I'm not a babysitter, I'm a mentor. I'll read with your kids, make healthy meals, clean up after, have them in bed on time, no screen time, teach ABCs." Brunson's reaction: "I would pay you $20/hour right now." The offer stack turned a commodity into a premium service.
- The iPhone auction example: Nobody in the audience would pay $600 for Brunson's used iPhone. Then he revealed what was on it: $750K+ of marketing courses (many out of print/impossible to find), contact list of Tony Robbins/Richard Branson/top influencers, access to all social platforms with 1M+ followers, email access to every vendor. The auction went from zero hands at $600 to six people willing to pay $500,000. Same physical product — the offer made it worth 800x more.
- Test whether an offer is irresistible by the "Dave test": Brunson pitches an idea to his team. If Dave says "Oh, that's a good idea" calmly — it's bad. If Dave says "Oh my gosh, that's amazing!" — it passes. The reaction should be visceral, not polite.
- Look at your market and identify what everyone else is offering. If your offer is indistinguishable from competitors, you're a commodity. Stack unique bonuses, exclusive access, or proprietary frameworks until your offer exists in a category of one.

KEY QUOTES:
- "It has to be an overwhelming amount of value for the right price."
- "I always try to make sure that it's worth ten times what I'm charging for it."
- "Why would you — two minutes ago you wouldn't pay 600 bucks for this thing — and he said, 'Based on what's on that phone, that's actually a really really good deal.'"

APPLY WHEN:
Use this when a client is competing on price, when conversion rates are low despite good traffic and messaging, or during offer creation workshops. The babysitter example is an excellent teaching tool for clients who think they "just sell X." The iPhone auction is the definitive illustration of how offer stacking transforms perceived value.

NOTE: This video is partially vlog-style (travel footage, visiting Joel Marion) with the offer content delivered in a casual conversational format. The two core examples (babysitter, iPhone auction) contain the real payload.

TITLE: How To Attract Customers: The 3 Keys To Marketing
VIDEO: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j7grJCOVoi4
CATEGORY: Strategy / Mass Movements
---

CORE CONCEPT:
Every successful business, brand, and community is built on a mass movement — a group of people moving toward a shared identity and future. Creating a mass movement requires three components: an Attractive Character (charismatic leader), a Future-Based Cause, and a New Opportunity. The critical distinction is between "improvement offers" (which require people to admit they suck at something) and "new opportunity offers" (which invite them to switch vehicles entirely).

TACTICAL TAKEAWAYS:
- The Mass Movement Triangle: (1) Attractive Character / Charismatic Leader, (2) Future-Based Cause, (3) New Opportunity. All three legs are required — missing any one and the "table" tips over.
- Attractive Character: In the connection economy, people associate with humans more than brands. ClickFunnels beats competitors partly because there's a human (Brunson) people feel connected to through books and stories. Even introverts (Kennedy, Brunson himself) can be attractive characters — it's about being authentic and relatable, not extroverted.
- Future-Based Cause: Every movement needs a collective destination. "This is where we're going as a group." Think about any community, religion, or brand you're part of — there's always a shared future being worked toward.
- New Opportunity vs. Improvement Offer (most important distinction): Improvement offers ("become better/faster/stronger") require the buyer to admit they're bad at something (fighting pride) and appeal only to the 2-3% of the population with real ambition. New Opportunity offers ("switch from your current vehicle to this better vehicle") only require desire — which everyone has. The framework: identify what vehicle your prospect is currently in to reach their goal, then prove that vehicle is inferior and present yours as the replacement.
- The "Opportunity Switch" in practice: At a 10X Growth Con audience wanting to grow their businesses, Brunson didn't say "let me improve your marketing." He said "whatever you're currently doing to grow your business isn't working — the new vehicle you need is funnels." This framing closed 10-20%+ of rooms. Map it: [Prospect's current vehicle] is broken > [Your new opportunity] is the replacement.
- ClickFunnels' positioning is itself a new opportunity: not "improve your website" but "websites are dead, funnels are the future." This is why it created a movement rather than just acquiring users.

KEY QUOTES:
- "I don't make a presentation. I'm looking at what are they trying to do right now to get the result — and I'm just trying to prove to them that whatever thing they're trying to do sucks."
- "While everybody has desire, maybe two to three percent of the population actually has ambition."
- "If I can make them that switch, then I got them 100% in line, hook and sinker."

APPLY WHEN:
Use this when positioning a client's offer in the market — especially if they're currently selling an "improvement" (better ads, faster results, more leads) and struggling with conversion. Reframe as a new opportunity: "stop doing X, start doing Y instead." Also use when building a brand strategy — ensure all three legs of the mass movement triangle are in place.
